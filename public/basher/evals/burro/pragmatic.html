P1Path to first paying customerPass
40+ customers across 6 countries with 300+ robots deployed and over 300,000 autonomous hours logged. The first paying customer question is long answered — the challenge now is second and third wave growth.

Not explicitly named in public materials. Growing from 300 to 3,000+ deployed units requires either a major dealer network expansion or a direct sales force at scale — neither is described with specificity in press materials. Manufacturing capacity and lead time per robot is also unknown.
P3Operating cash required (3/6/12 months)Incomplete
Not disclosed. With 44 employees and hardware manufacturing, monthly operating cash is likely in the $400K–$700K range, but the company has not made this public. The Series B of $24M implies 18–36 months of runway at those burn levels, but no explicit schedule exists.
P4Capital expenditure required (3/6/12 months)Incomplete
Hardware company with growing product line (original Burro, Burro Verde, Burro Grande, expansion packs) — CapEx for tooling, manufacturing scale-up, and inventory is material but undisclosed.
P5Survival runway without new fundingIncomplete
$24M Series B with undisclosed burn rate makes this impossible to calculate precisely. At 44 employees plus hardware COGS and manufacturing, the company is almost certainly not default alive. Runway is likely 18–30 months from the Series B close — acceptable but dependent on continued fundraising.
P6First critical hireIncomplete
Not identified in public materials. At Series B stage with 44 employees, the most likely blocking hire is a VP of Sales or Chief Revenue Officer capable of structuring the dealer network and enterprise sales motion needed to go from 300 to 3,000 units.
P7Unit economics todayPass
Customer-facing unit economics are documented: ROI under 2 months for most operations, with one robot enabling a 48% increase in worker productivity in table grapes. Internal unit economics (BOM cost, margin per robot) are not disclosed, but the customer ROI case is real and repeatable across multiple deployments.
P8Single point of failureIncomplete
Not named. Obvious candidates: component supply chain concentration (robots require sensors, compute, actuators from a small number of suppliers), dealer channel dependency, or the possibility that a major OEM (Deere, CNH) decides to build a competing product and leverage its existing dealer network.
P9Regulatory or legal gatePass
Autonomous outdoor robots hauling alongside human workers carry minimal regulatory burden. No FDA, EPA, or USDA approval gates apply. Safety standards for co-working robots (ISO 10218) exist but are manageable. The regulatory landscape is a genuine advantage over chemical or pesticide-based competitors.
P10Revenue concentration riskIncomplete
40+ customers across 6 countries is encouraging diversification, but the largest customers (Petitti Family Farms, Altman Plants, Sierra Gold Nurseries) are named prominently, suggesting they represent a material portion of the deployed fleet. Exact revenue concentration is not disclosed.
P11Founder proof of executionPass
The execution record speaks for itself: 300+ deployed robots, 300,000+ autonomous hours, 40+ customers in 6 countries, $44.5M raised from Toyota Ventures and institutional investors. Whatever the founder background story, the operational track record is demonstrated.
